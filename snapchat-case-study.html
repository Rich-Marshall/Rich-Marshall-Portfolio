Snapchat Case Study

This is a case study for the popular photo messaging app, Snapchat. The case study focuses on Snapchat's unconventional approach to UX and their attempts to block older generations from the app.

Snapchat and Facebook’s own Instagram have been the apps that most Facebook leavers have turned to. Snapchat took a very different approach to UX design than most companies do. They have made their app almost deliberately hard to navigate and this plays right into the psychological motivations of young users. “It is their baffling UX that maintains Snapchat’s relevance to their key demographic: teens and millennials. This target audience is as powerful as it is fickle, and capturing it means keeping Snapchat cool. And nothing kills cool faster than adults”. Snapchat appeals to teenagers and young adults and simultaneously solves the parent problem. Poor UX catches up. This has been observed from their “flatlining” user figures. However, this may not be a bad thing for snapchat. Ethical or not, their new plan is to ensure each user’s time on the app is maximised.

The Problem

Snapchat is specifically targeted to younger users (around the same age range as my Vibes app was targeted). This presents a problem as the current shift in some social media platforms, namely Facebook, has been an influx of older generations. The real issue for these platforms comes in the form of parents. As parents flock to a platform, the younger generations leave.

The Solution

Snapchat has employed an incredibly unique tactic in order to preserve their younger audience. As touched on before, this is because younger users leave the platform as their parents join. Snapchat has instead deliberately implemented a difficult UX. This may seem counter intuitive to a lot of UX designers. However, it works for the most part. Younger users will spend the time to learn a difficult User Experience and their parents won’t. This in turn (a move that has been widely criticised) has ensured that those users who spend the time to learn the UI/UX will then continue to invest more time per week in using the app. However, while this works very well in the medium term, it may not work so well in the long term as can be seen by their flatlining user figures. The takeaway here is that while this may at first appear a great strategy, it may be more beneficial to dial back the number of instances of frustrating UX in order to provide some respite for those who persevere, while not over encouraging older generations to flock to the app.
